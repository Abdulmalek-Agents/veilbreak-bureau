## 2. The Bull Case — Why This Can Win

**Senior Game Designer's view:**
The pitch correctly identifies a genuine 20-year-old design problem in the genre: hand-authored mysteries die on one playthrough. Every detective-game subreddit has the same top thread — *"Now what?"* Veilbreak's three-pass generator (World → Crime → Evidence) is architecturally sound and mirrors the Wave Function Collapse / constraint-solver approach that has worked in *Caves of Qud*, *Dwarf Fortress*, and *Bad News*. The diegetic Pinboard with green/red/yellow feedback is genuinely novel and solves the "did I just guess?" problem that plagued *L.A. Noire* and the modern *Sherlock Holmes* games.

**Community & Marketing view:**
This game is *built for streaming.* The 25–45 min case length matches a single Twitch chat session perfectly. The accusation moment is a guaranteed clip. The "wrong accusation has consequences" hook is a story journalists *will* write about. We project Day-1 wishlists of 80–150k if marketing is competent.

**Business Analyst view:**
The comp basket is excellent. *Return of the Obra Dinn* (2M+ units), *The Case of the Golden Idol* + *Rise of the Golden Idol* (combined 1M+), *Shadows of Doubt* (650k+), *Disco Elysium* (5M+) — together they prove the TAM. The premium-indie price point ($24.99 EA / $29.99 1.0) is correct. iPad and Switch ports are *not optional* — they typically add 30–50% to lifetime revenue for this genre.

## 3. The Bear Case — Why This Can Lose

**Producer/Tech Director view:**
The pitch underestimates the writing budget by an order of magnitude. The doc casually says "Tracery-grade prose only" and "hire 3+ narrative designers." Realistically, to make 200+ NPCs feel authored across 30+ case archetypes you need a *writing room*, not three writers — closer to 6–10 narrative designers working for 24–30 months, plus an editor, plus an automated solver, plus a QA team that plays 10,000+ generated cases. Budget: **$3.5–6M minimum** before art. The doc implies a sub-$2M project.

**Hostile Reviewer view (devil's advocate):**
- *Shadows of Doubt* tried this. It sold 650k+ but its #1 negative review pattern on Steam is literally **"the cases feel hollow and repetitive after 10 hours."** That game is the cautionary tale that the pitch never seriously engages with.
- The "every case is logically solvable" promise is **harder than landing a SpaceX rocket.** Verifying solvability for a procedural narrative game requires an automated solver that proves a *unique* solution exists from clues a human can reach via *legitimate* inference. The pitch waves at this with "fail-test 10,000 cases" — that's not a mitigation, that's a vibe.
- The roguelike audience is *not* the detective audience. The pitch assumes one Venn diagram; it's actually two adjacent ones. *Inscryption* and *Slay the Spire* players overlap with *Obra Dinn* players at maybe 15%, not the implied 60%.
- "Procedural prose" is the AAA holy grail. *Versu*, *Bad News*, *Façade*, *Storyteller* — all the prior art either shipped tiny scope or shipped to mixed reception.

**Senior Game Designer's view (counter-bull):**
The doc's risk table dismisses the "procedural feels mechanical" risk as "High → mitigated by hiring narrative designers." That's the equivalent of saying "the risk of the rocket exploding is mitigated by hiring good engineers." It is the *entire* project.

## 5. Critical Risks (Ranked by Severity)

1. **THE PROSE PROBLEM (existential):** Procedural mysteries that don't *read* as authored will be reviewed as "AI-feeling" — a death sentence post-2024. The doc mentions Tracery; this is insufficient. Need a hybrid system: hand-authored *case beats* with procedural *parameter binding* (the *Wildermyth* / *Failbetter Games* model). Without this re-architecture, the project fails on launch reviews.
2. **THE FAIRNESS GUARANTEE (technical):** "Every case logically solvable" requires an automated solver. Writing a solver that handles the world graph, NPC social network, and clue distribution is a 6–12 month dedicated R&D track *before* content production starts. The pitch buries this in a single line.
3. **THE WRITING BUDGET (financial):** $3.5–6M actual writing/editorial cost vs. implied <$2M total budget. The math doesn't math.
4. **THE GENRE OVERLAP MIRAGE (audience):** Detective fans and roguelike fans are not the same buyer. Marketing must position to *both* with different messaging or lose half the audience.
5. **THE STREAMER DOUBLE-EDGED SWORD (marketing):** Streamability is great for launch — but mystery games suffer the most "spoiler decay" of any genre. Once one big streamer solves a case live, half of viewers won't buy it. Procedural mitigates this *if* prose holds up — see Risk 1.
6. **THE OCCULT LAYER (scope creep):** Mundane + Veil-touched + True Veilbreak = three tonal modes the writing team must master simultaneously. Cut one or two for v1.0; keep them for expansions.
7. **iPAD UX FEASIBILITY (platform):** The pitch correctly identifies iPad as critical but the Pinboard UI must be designed touch-first from day one, not ported. Many indie ports fail this and lose 30–40% of forecast revenue.

## 6. Recommendations to Increase Success Probability

### 🔴 Must-do before any content production
1. **Re-architect the writing pipeline as hybrid authored-procedural.** Build a *storylet library* (Failbetter Games / Sunless Sea / Cultist Simulator pattern). 400–800 hand-authored prose fragments with procedural parameter bindings. This is the only path that has shipped successfully.
2. **Build the automated case-solver in pre-production.** Six-month dedicated R&D milestone with clear pass/fail gate. If solver can't guarantee unique solutions in 99.9% of generated cases, **stop the project.**
3. **Vertical slice with brutal honesty.** Build 1 perfect case (24+ hours of designer love poured into a procedural shell) and put it in front of 50 *Obra Dinn* superfans. If 60%+ describe the prose as "feeling authored," continue. If not, iterate or pivot.

### 🟡 Should-do during production
4. **Reduce ambition from 30–50 cases to 12 hand-tuned case archetypes** with deep procedural variation per archetype. This is the *Slay the Spire* model (limited card pool, deep combinatorial depth) and it works.
5. **Cut the occult layer for v1.0.** Ship pure detective. Add Veil-touched cases as the Year 1 free DLC drop — this gives marketing a beat and de-risks tone.
6. **Hire a senior writer-director from interactive fiction.** Names to court: Emily Short, Jon Ingold (Inkle), Alexis Kennedy (Failbetter alumni), Hannah Nicklin. One of these onboard de-risks the prose problem 50%.
7. **Build the Daily Case mode as a first-class feature.** This is your retention engine. It is *also* your free marketing engine. Treat it like *Balatro*'s daily challenge, not a side feature.
8. **Streaming-mode toggle.** Spoiler-free chat-friendly mode that obfuscates suspect names with codenames for streamers. Active marketing flywheel.

### 🟢 Optional accelerators
9. **Daily Case subscription is risky.** Players resent subscriptions in premium indies. Make it free tier + cosmetic "Detective's Annual" cosmetic pass instead.
10. **Skip console launch entirely for Year 1.** Steam + iPad first, Switch Year 2, PS5/Xbox Year 3. Console parity costs ~$400k in QA you can't afford in EA.
11. **ARG-style alternate ending.** One case across the entire playerbase reveals a community-solvable ARG that culminates in a special ending. This is *low cost, high mythic value* — see *Inscryption*'s success.

## 8. Closing Statement from the Panel

This is **the most intellectually interesting pitch in your portfolio.** It targets a real, unsolved design problem with a coherent technical thesis. We rate it the *highest-ceiling, highest-variance* of your three projects.

Ship this game and you don't just have a hit — you have a licensable engine, three sequels, and a permanent footprint in game history. Ship this game *badly* and you become a footnote in the same paragraph as *Versu* and *Façade*.

**Our recommendation: Greenlight the R&D phase only.** Spend 6–9 months building the solver and the storylet system. Vertical-slice one perfect case. Then make the full-production call with real data. Do not commit to full production based on the pitch alone.
